PERIPHERAL ARTERIAL DISEASE (PAD) RESULTS
PERIPHERAL ARTERIAL DISEASE (PAD)
Peripheral Artery Disease occurs when atherosclerosis (plaque build-up) occurs in the lower extremities. This condition can cause many symptoms such as pain while walking, leg weakness and numbness, discoloration and leg/ankle sores. If left untreated, PAD can cause other serious issues such as gangrene; in fact, the presence of plaque also increases the risk of heart attack. The PAD test measures the difference in blood pressures between the upper and lower extremities, identifying the possibility of PAD. Your ABI number is calculated by dividing the right or left ankle by the higher arm pressure.

HELICOBACTER PYLORI RESULTS
HELICOBACTER PYLORI

Helicobacter pylori, or H. pylori, is a spiral-shaped bacterium that grows in the mucus layer that coats the inside of the human stomach and burrows into the mucus layer, which is less acidic than the inside space, or lumen, of the stomach. H. pylori can also attach to the cells that line the inner surface of the stomach.

H. pylori infection is a major cause of gastric (stomach) cancer, specifically non-cardia gastric cancer (cancer in all areas of the stomach, except for the top portion near where it joins the esophagus). H. pylori infection also causes gastric mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) lymphoma.

It is also a major risk factor for peptic ulcer disease and is responsible for the majority of ulcers of the stomach and upper small intestine. More information about H. pylori and peptic ulcer disease is available from the National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases.

Rinne and Weber tests are exams that test for hearing loss. They determine whether a patient has conductive or sensorineural hearing loss. PSA RESULTS

What is prostate cancer screening?

PSA stands for “prostate specific antigen”. (PSA is a protein made by the prostate and can be measured with a simple blood. The PSA test screens for early detection of prostate cancer and other prostate conditions.

Who should be screened for prostate cancer?

Prostate cancer screening is done in men who have no symptoms of the disease. In most cases, men should start discussing prostate cancer screening around the age of 50. Men with risk factors for prostate cancer (such as black men or men with a family history of prostate cancer) might want to begin screening at age 40 to 45. Most experts recommend against prostate screening for men age 70 or older, or for men with serious health problems.


What does my test report show?

The report below indicates if your PSA blood test results are normal or abnormal.

If your PSA level is abnormal, do not panic.

It's possible your PSA is high for reasons unrelated to cancer such as an enlarged prostate, a prostate infection or injury, as well as ejaculations(orgasms).

It is important for you to schedule an appointment with your health care provider to discuss your PSA test results regardless of whether they are NORMAL or ABNORMAL. Your health care provider will decide if additional testing is required and/or when you should have your next PSA screening test done.
